# Scene 001 — the man who walked out
*Played 2026-07-07 · Mode A (Hannu plays the Sealed One) · In-world: dawn, first day of the Rekindling — the ruin of Berzhár · Present: the Sealed One (unnamed), a young Warden of the Ḥatumín*

*Running transcript — appended as played. Verbatim; Hannu's lines never paraphrased. `⭐` marks precious lines. Footer added at distill.*

---

**GM:**

*Previously: Eight hundred and twelve years you slept beneath Berzhár, sealed at lips and hands and eyes, while the mountain drank your light. Then gold came walking into your dream, and you woke, and spoke, and the word cost what such words cost. The mountain split. That much is written on the world. Almost nothing is written in you.*

Morning. Šemmá stands new above the eastern ridge and the light lies on your face like a warm hand.

You are walking down a slope of ruin, and you do not remember beginning to walk. Behind you Berzhár lies open — split stem to crown, a canyon of black and rust still settling somewhere deep, with long groans like a ship. Iron dust falls fine as grey snow. Where it lands on your skin it drinks: each fleck a small cold sip at the light in you, and the light in you is low. Your raiment — gold, glass, woven air; you do not remember speaking it — is guttering like a lamp at the end of its oil.

In your left hand, a book. Heavy. Warm. Your fingers ache from how long they have gripped it.

And behind your breastbone, a tilt — the one certain thing you carry. Somewhere west and south, under the haze of the world, something is newly bright. Newborn. Wounded. Bleeding light the way you bleed light. Every step not toward it feels uphill.

Below you on the shattered stair a voice is chanting. A young man in Warden grey lies pinned at the thigh under a slide of black brick, blood in the dust around him, and he is saying the old formula at you — cracked, dogged, over and over, as if a sentence could hold what a mountain could not:

"Be-berzá pe-qol ḥatmut — pe-ḥatúm ló šahár — be-berzá pe-qol ḥatmut —"

He does not stop as you come down. His eyes never leave you.

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

I set down the book and go to him. I'm going to see if I can help him to get out from under the brick.

I don't know who I am but I know that I am someone who helps people.

**GM:**

The book stays warm in the dust behind you, like a coal that will not die.

The chant stumbles when your shadow falls across the boy. Up close he is young — seventeen, perhaps — a carrier's leather yoke still on his shoulders, his spilled load scattered down the stair. He presses back against the stone.

"Don't speak." His voice cracks. "Please. Don't say anything on me."

The slab across his thigh is the purified black, forge-marked with a struck-through ray. When your hands close on it, cold climbs into your wrists; the iron sips at you through your palms, patient as a drain. The strength you find is a starved man's strength. You take the weight anyway. As you strain, your raiment goes out — not torn: extinguished, gold to grey to gone — and you are a naked man in the dawn wind, lifting stone.

The slab rolls. The boy screams. His leg comes free wrong-shaped, blood welling dark and fast into the dust.

He stares — at your hands, at the grey prints the iron left on them. His lips still move: "…pe-ḥatúm ló šahár…" Then he stops himself, and says, small and human:

"Brother Ossen was on the stair below me. I can't hear him anymore."

Down the mountain, a bell is tolling. Cracked. Uneven.

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

I note to show that I understand. I pass my fingers across my lips to show that I'm not going to speak and then I put my hand to my heart. I examine his leg. Do I know what's wrong with it? I feel like I should remember something about human bodies and how to treat them.

**GM:**

When your fingers rise toward your lips the boy goes rigid — to him a bare hand moving with intent is the second channel, the ʾot; they chained your hands for eight hundred years against exactly this. Then the hand settles on your heart, and he understands, and something in his face breaks open.

"You— it understands—" He catches himself. "*You.* Forgive me. You understand me."

The leg. Your hands go to it and the knowledge is simply there, worn smooth with use: the great bone of the thigh is broken mid-length — the muscle around it already swelling tight — and the brick's edge has opened a gash a hand deep. The blood is the dark, steady kind, not the bright leaping kind. You know that this matters. You know the leg must be drawn straight, bound hard, splinted, or he dies on this stair.

What is gone is everything around the knowing — whose hands taught yours, whose leg it was the first time. Reaching for that is stepping on a stair that isn't there.

Near him: his grey robe. The leather straps of his yoke. The yoke's wooden ribs, scattered.

The boy has begun to shiver. His lips are going the color of the dust.

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

I grip his shoulder, squeeze it hard, look into his eyes to show that this is going to communicate, this is going to hurt. I gesture to show what I'm going to do and then when he's ready, if he's ready, I'll just do it.

**GM:**

He holds your eyes. Whatever he finds there, it is not what his catechism promised him, and you watch him decide to trust it anyway.

"Wait." He drags a yoke-strap between his teeth. Then, around the leather, muffled, he begins the formula again — not at you this time, at himself, the way the Order binds its own: "be-berzá… pe-qol… ḥatmut…" He nods once.

You brace your heel against the stair-lip, take his ankle in both grey-printed hands, and pull. The muscle fights you; the bone grinds, shifts, seats itself with a sound you feel through his body rather than hear. He screams through the strap — one long note — and goes out like a lamp.

It is easier then. You pack the gash with wadded robe, bind it hard with the straps, lash two yoke-ribs along the thigh. The dark seep slows under the cloth. Your hands are shaking, filmed with his blood; the dust drinks the drops that fall.

Šemmá clears the ridge. The warmth on your bare back is almost kind.

Then the bell below stops, mid-toll. In the new silence: the mountain settling, the boy's shallow breath — and from the stair below, where Brother Ossen was, nothing at all.

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

I look around. I look at the landscape. Is there anywhere I could carry the young brother to get him to safety? Is there a way down and also where is this feeling of incompleteness pulling me?

And do I have the strength to carry him?

**GM:**

You straighten, and for the first time you see the world you broke back into.

The stair falls away below you in long ruined stretches — whole flights standing, then gaps of raw black scree where the mountain's splitting shook them down. Two turns below, a slide of brick has buried the stair entirely. That is where Ossen is. Nothing there moves.

At the mountain's foot, perhaps an hour's hard descent: a walled cloister, grey stone and black roofs, forge-chimneys cold. One wing has been crushed by a thrown slab the size of a barn. Small figures cluster in the forecourt around the silenced bell — and now a thin line of them detaches and begins to climb the stair toward you. Eight, ten. Too far to see their faces, or what they carry.

Beyond the cloister the foothills fall to a brown plain: a straight road, a river with a stone bridge, and far off a smudge of coal-smoke standing over some town. Past all of it, southwest, the sky is dirty with distance — and that is where the tilt in your chest points. Not to the town. Through it. Beyond the horizon, steady as a lodestone, indifferent to bells and blood.

Strength — you have it, barely. The body answers, but the well under it is shallow, and there is a hunger in you with no bottom to it at all. You could carry him. It would take everything you presently are.

Behind you, the book waits in the dust, patient, warm.

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

So I'm guessing it's a choice between the boy and the book. I glance at the book.

I'm also suddenly worried that the monks coming from down there don't have good intentions and if they're coming here then there's probably no point in carrying the boy, if he's going to be all right for a little while. Is there any way down without meeting the monks?

**GM:**

The book lies where you set it, and now you truly look at it: bound in something like leather that is not leather, the color of late honey, no title anywhere. The grey dust falls on everything alike — but where a fleck lands on the book it does not rest. It sinks, softly, like a snowflake into warm water, and is gone. Each one that lands takes something with it. The book is being eaten. Slowly — years slowly, maybe — but this mountain has nothing but dust, and time.

The boy: his breathing is shallow and even, the seep under the strap-binding stopped. An hour will not kill him. Nothing about him is certain beyond that.

The climbers have gained a flight. They come in single file, in order, no scrambling — discipline, not panic. Two of them carry something long between them; at this distance a litter and a ram are the same shape. And now the wind turns and brings their sound up the stair, thin and rhythmic. They are chanting as they climb.

Ways down: the stair is the only made one. The mountain's western flank is trackless — rust-cliff and scree, no path, a full day of hard broken country before any road, and no man could carry another down it. It would hide you. It would also mean the mountain stands between you and everyone who saw you walk out of it.

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

If there's any piece of the monk's robe you he can spare, I will wrap the book in it to protect it from the iron dust, ideally in a makeshift bag I can carry or put on my back, and then I try to find a way down that avoids the monks. if there is anything the boy can spare to hide my nakedness, that might also be useful

**GM:**

The boy wears three layers against the mountain: a short hooded dust-cloak of oiled wool, the grey over-robe — a third of it already gone into his leg — and an under-tunic. You leave him the robe and the warmth; you take the cloak, and a wide strip torn from the robe's ruined hem. On his belt you find a carrier's kit: a water-skin, half a black loaf in waxed cloth, a small iron work-knife that sips cold at your fingers through its handle.

The wool settles on your shoulders — the first clothing in eight hundred years that something else made for some other body, smelling of sweat and lanolin and boy. You wrap the book and sling it at your back with a spare yoke-strap. Through the cloth, between your shoulder blades, its warmth rests like a hand.

As you cinch the strap, he surfaces — eyes half-open, fever-bright, watching you dress yourself in Warden grey. He doesn't reach for you. He whispers:

"What do I tell them? Who do I say came out?"

His eyes stay on you as long as they can, and then they don't.

Below, the chant has words now, faint but climbing. To the west, off the stair's edge, the broken country waits — rust and shadow, and no path at all.

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

When the boy asks his question, I hesitate, and then say, "A friend." I leave the knife on the ground next to him, and set down on the broken slope.

**GM:**

Your voice comes out a ruin — a hinge unopened for eight centuries — and the two words cost like coins. In the dawn cold your breath hangs faintly gold.

The boy does not flinch. He looks at you the way men look at the sea. Then, testing the weight of it, the way it will sound in the telling:

"A friend came out of the mountain."

His eyes close. His hand finds the knife you set by it, and holds on.

You step off the stair's edge into the broken west. The scree takes you at once — rust plates shifting underfoot, every third step a small betrayal, dust rising around your knees to sip and sip at you. Behind, the chant swells, nears the boy, and then the mountain's black shoulder comes between and cuts it off like a door.

The west face lies in its own shadow still, cold as a cellar. The tilt in your chest pulls southwest, across your path, so that every step down this hidden side is half uphill against it. You go anyway — a small grey figure in a dead land, glowing at the seams, a warm book riding your back like a hand between your shoulders.
