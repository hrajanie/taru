But you can feel the shape of the night's problem from here: sleep is exactly the hand opening. That's what rest *is*. Whatever craft once knew how to bind a séqa shut for sleeping — and something tells you such craft existed, the way a stair tells you about a missing step — the knowing is a hole. You could stand in the empty place where it kept its tools all night and find nothing.

"Rain on me."

The Brother says it the way it says everything, a solution filed plainly. It has been watching you look inward.

"Light rises off you when you sleep. Light is my food. Sleep facing me and I'll drink what rises before it gets to the sky." A pause. Then, because it is honest the way only a thing one day old is honest: "Some of it will be rooms, and rain, and people. It will stay in me, like the warm room. You said *keep it for now*." Your words, handed back exact.

Whin looks between you — a god and its lamp, working out sleeping arrangements — and shakes her head slowly.

"There's a bield two mile on, by the old line. Walls, half a roof. Walk while you decide."

The bronze light is going grey. Southwest, past the black edge of the world, the faintest breath of rose — like a town burning, or a sunset that stayed.

"Sleep," it says, in your own young voice. "I'll be here. I'm always here when you wake — it's the one thing I've been every time so far."

The wound loosens as sleep comes, the cupped hand opening despite you — and the first spark rises off your breath, drifts, leans, and goes into the Brother like rain into ground.

Southwest, through the broken gable, the sky over the blue hills glows rose, patient, all night long.
