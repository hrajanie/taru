**GM:**

*Previously: On your first free night the qadín came into your dream and charged you — the newborn is a lamp in unpracticed hands; go and set what is broken. Morning brought Whin, gleaning your fallen dream-light off the scree, and in a dry beck the largest remnant of all: a man-shape, which you fed from your own wound until it woke wearing your face and your lost voice. One debt sworn, one mountain behind you, two hours southwest — Harrick's shieling, and a latch that has been touched.*

Whin eases the door open with the pole and lets the morning in ahead of her.

Nobody. One room: hearth, plank bed, a stool, shelves with the summer gear. But the air says *lived in* — tallow smoke and wet wool, hours old, not months. She goes in reading it like ground.

"Blanket's folded wrong. Uncle folds in thirds." She crouches at the hearth, holds her palm over the ash, and looks up at you, flat. "Warm under. Someone slept here last night and went this morning."

On the hearthstone, square in the middle where no one could miss them: two coins, left as payment in the country way. She picks one up and her thumb stops on it.

"That's not cloister money." She holds it out to you. Heavy silver-grey, milled edge, clean strike: a tower on one face and on the other a sunburst — with a bar struck through it.

The Brother, at your shoulder, looks at the coin and then out the open door, southwest.

"They went the way we're going," it says, in your voice.

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

"What's that way," I ask Whin.

**GM:**

"The Rake." She tips her head at the long haze past the door. "Drove road, down under the fells. Southwest it runs two days to Sanding — market town, where the wool goes, where I'd sell—" she stops herself naming it with the Brother standing there. "Past Sanding it's low country and the Sund, and past *that* it's the old glass, where nothing grips. Nobody's from past Sanding."

You take the coin. It sips at your fingers, faint and cold — iron under the silver color. And you know the mark without knowing how: the bar through the sun is the seal-brick's mark, the struck-through ray, the sign they chained you under — but here it's cut proud and bright, like a boast.

Whin watches your face. "Good money, though," she says, and takes it back, and pockets both coins with steading arithmetic — her roof, her silver. "Whoever slept here paid fair and folded the blanket. That's more manners than most."

She stands the pole by the door. "I'll go down for bread and clothes before the light's long. Two hours there, two back. You'll still be here when I come, or you won't — but the debt walks with you either way."

She waits, one eyebrow up, to hear which it will be.

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

"We'll be here." I take a deep breath. "And Whin? Thank you. There is more to you than thorns." I'm not sure why, but I trust her. Perhaps it's the oath.

**GM:**

The thorn line lands somewhere she didn't armor. She looks at you a beat too long, then covers it the way she covers everything.

"There's more to everyone than what they sell." She shoulders her sack. "Bar the door. Anyone comes up the Rake side, you're two shepherd brothers out of Coldshoulder counting the fold walls. Talk as little as you look."

At the doorway she pauses. "And don't burn anything of mine for warmth. I've seen how you make a fire."

Then she's gone down the fell with the goat, small and quick, not looking back — visible a long time in that bare country, then folded into it.

Quiet settles. The shieling smells of tallow and old wool and someone else's careful night. The Brother stands in the middle of the room, exactly where it stopped, cloaked to the chin, its light a faint moving line under the skin of its throat.

"She feeds the goat," it says, as if adding a fact to a very short list of things it knows about the world. Then it looks at you.

"You're hungry. I can feel it from here — like standing near a door with cold behind it. What do we do about that, brother?"

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

"What are we going to do about you?" I say. "My hunger will keep. Do … you eat? Other than the radiance."

Without waiting for a response, I continue. "You might as well make yourself useful. So what she said and keep watch. I'm going to try to find out about more of us and where we came from."

Then I unwrap the book.

**GM:**

"I don't think I'm hollow the right way for bread," the Brother says, considering it seriously. "There's nowhere in me for it to go. What you gave me is what I have — I can feel the level of it, like water in a jar." It says this without complaint, a fact for the list.

It goes to the shutter and sets its eye to the gap, obedient — though as you unwrap the book its head turns, just once, the way a hungry man's head turns in a kitchen doorway. Then it makes itself look at the fell.

The book, unwrapped, in your lap. Warm through your trousers like a sleeping animal. Up close in daylight you can see the toll the mountain took: the honey-colored cover is pocked with tiny grey scars, each one a fleck of dust that sank and fed.

You open it.

The pages aren't paper. Same not-leather stuff, thin as skin, and the writing on them isn't ink — it's *in* the leaf, faint light in the shapes of rayed script, columns running top to bottom. The dots ride high on their rays, nearly all of them: whatever this is, it is written bright. Here and there a letter is broken mid-stroke — the wounded aspect — and here and there a word is simply gone, a grey scar where the dust drank it.

You can read it. Like the leg, like the hearth: the *how* is there, whole; the *where-from* a missing stair.

The first column, in a hand that must be no hand at all:

*To the one who walks out: you chose this, twice — do not go looking for the man who chose. He is spent, and he was ~~——~~ anyway. Feed the body first. Trust the tilt. And keep this book from iron, because I have put ~~———~~ in it, and it is all that ~~——~~ of us.*

The next words run under your finger, waiting.

**GM:**

"I don't think sleep can find me — there's no dark in me for it to come in through," the Brother says. "I'll watch. And, brother — when you dream, you shed. Late light through the open door. Whin is setting things out of her sack onto the stool — a loaf, a crock, a bundle of clothes — with her back deliberately to the Brother, who stands exactly where it stood, watching you wake.

"Your uncle's coming," Whin says, not turning around. "Not today. But he's coming, and I need to tell you why before you eat that."

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

"The Wardens said the boy was dead?" I ask. "I'm sorry you had to lie for me, Whin. I'm sorry you have to leave your home."

**GM:**

"No — the talking boy's alive. Leg in splints, telling his *friend* story to anyone the Order can't shut up fast enough." She saws another slice. "The dead one's another brother. Ossen, they said — dug him from under a stair this morning, or what the stair left. That's the corpse in the sermon. By the time it got to our table the two had run together: dead boy, stolen cloak, the thing wearing it." A humorless twist of her mouth. "Stories braid fast when they're frightened."

"And don't be sorry on my account. You didn't put the lie in my mouth; I did that myself, with the coin already in my pocket." She jerks her chin at the bundle of clothes. **GM:**

The knowing comes up the way the leg-craft came: shape first, source gone. The Ḥatumín bind themselves before they bind anything else — that's what the name means, the sealed ones keeping the Sealed; every Warden wears his own vow like a chain. And the vow runs: *no brother's blood, no brother's breaking.* They do not whip and they do not hang their own. What they do — the fragment surfaces cold and specific — is *seal*: a transgressor's lips bound with the same rite they lay on the mountains, for a term, or for life. Not harmed. *Quieted.* You feel the truth of it the way you felt the thigh-bone seat: this was once something you knew from very near.

How much of it survived eight hundred years of the Long Quiet, you have no way to say.

"Whatever they do to their own, they'll do it softly and behind walls," you find yourself saying, and Whin files away the fact that you'd know, the way she files everything.

She points her knife at the three drops laid on the grey cloth, changing the subject like closing a door. "Terms were: small things that fall are mine." A pause; the honesty costs her visibly. "Those fell indoors, though. Under my roof, off *your* sleep. I'll hear an argument."

Outside the open door the light is going long and orange. Uncle Harrick and his Wardens are twelve hours away.

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

I look at Brother. "Would these --" I point at the glass things -- "make you stronger for the journey?" I look at Whin. "My debt will be paid with greater coin than these, I promise, and if I'm right, these are like bread for him."

**GM:**

Whin looks at the three drops a long moment — coin, walking away into a creature's chest. Then she flicks her hand, paid-in-full. "Feed it. I've not charged for bread yet, and I'm not starting with whatever *that* is." A beat. "Greater coin. I heard it. I'll name the debt when I'm ready and not before."

The Brother takes the first drop with both hands, careful as an egg, and presses it to its breastbone. The light doesn't pour — it *steps across*, the way a flame steps from wick to wick, and the emptied glass goes ash-grey and light as a husk. The seam in its chest runs brighter and slower. Something in its posture settles, fed.

"This one has rain in it," it says, wondering, holding the second. "Rain on a roof, from underneath. Yours?"

It eats all three. It doesn't ask again about the boy.

You dress in a dead man's clothes: wool shirt gone soft with washing, patched breeches, a herder's coat that smells of the same lanolin as everything in this country, and boots — worn to someone else's feet, but sound. Whin wraps the Brother's feet and shins in sacking and cord, herder-fashion, refusing to touch it more than the work needs.

By the time the door is barred behind you, the fell has gone blue-grey and the first stars are opening — the small far kind, the kind that stay put. Below, the dale is coming out in lights: steading after steading, more fires than a settled evening wants. The country is awake, and afraid, and talking to itself.

Whin sets off down the goat-path, sure-footed in the dark, the goat's bell muffled with a rag. Southwest, the way the land falls, the Rake runs pale into the last of the light.
