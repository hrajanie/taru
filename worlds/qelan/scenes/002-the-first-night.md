# Scene 002 — the first night
*Played 2026-07-07 / 2026-07-09 · Mode A (Hannu plays the Sealed One) · In-world: dusk of day 1 → morning of day 2, west flank of Berzhár · Present: the Sealed One, the qadín (dream), Whin, the Brother*

**GM:**

*Previously: At dawn you broke out of Berzhár with a book you don't remember and one certainty — somewhere southwest, a newborn wounded one bleeds light, and you must find them. You bought a boy's life back from under the iron, told him to call you a friend, and went down the hidden west face alone.*

Dusk, and the mountain is nearly done with you. All day it has been rust-plates and dead ravines, cliffs that turned you a mile out of your way, dust that rose to sip at you with every sliding step. The half loaf went at midday; it fell into your hunger like a pebble into a well. The water-skin is light now. Your legs have gone from pain to something past it, a dumb animal obedience.

Šemmá goes down red behind the western hills, and the light's leaving is like a hand lifted off your skin. The cold comes up out of the ground almost at once. And as the land darkens, you begin — faintly, at cuff and collar and the seams of you — to show.

On a shoulder of the slope ahead, black against the last light: a stump of a tower. An old watch-post of the Order, from centuries when they watched every face of this mountain — roofless, its door long gone, but its drum of dry stone still stands man-high, and inside there is a dead hearth, a stone cistern skinned with old rain, and no wind.

Far below, the plain has gone blue. On the straight road, a chain of small lights is crawling — toward the mountain, not away. Word is faster than you.

And sleep is coming for you now — not like rest, like weather. An undertow behind the eyes. You have not slept as a free man in eight hundred years, and the body means to, whether you choose it or not.

Overhead, one by one, the night's small far wounds are opening.

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

I seek shelter in the tower and finish the loaf. I dare not make light but I do need warmth and I do think the bodies need me. I think I might have a way to will the stones to be warm with a small cost but I feel like the body remembers something here on how to warm stone with radiance even without fire or light.

**GM:**

The tower takes you in like a dry well. Inside: the dead hearth, swallows' nests gone to powder in the wall-chinks, the cistern's skin of rain that tastes of stone and old sky when you fill the water-skin. The last crust goes in two bites, and your hunger closes over it without a ripple.

The hearthstones are cold as the underside of centuries. But when you lay your palms on them, the hands know. Not the why — the how: palms flat, a slow smoothing, the motion of easing a wrinkle from a bedsheet. Hearth-craft. Someone's rooms were once warmed this way. Reaching for whose is the stair-step that isn't there.

With the sign, the aperture — you feel it now, the first time since the mountain — opens a hair. Behind the hair's breadth is a sea. It leans on the crack like tide on a lock-gate, and it would love to pour, and some part of you whispers that opening is the only thing that ever felt like rest.

You let through warmth alone. The stones drink and give it back slow — bread-oven heat, no flame, no light but your own seams. Where your palms pressed, two hand-shapes stand paler in the dark stone, ash-pale: the stones spending themselves. And something of yours is spent too — a coin taken quietly from a purse already nearly empty. You cannot tell, yet, which one.

The warmth fills the little drum of the tower. Your body shakes once, hard — thaw-shaking — and eases. The book lies beside you, warm on its own account. Through the roofless top the sky's small wounds look in.

And the undertow arrives, patient and total, and begins to close over you like water.

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

I let the sleep take me and I dream of the Qadin.

**GM:**

Sleep takes you the way a river takes what falls into it: quickly, and toward something.

The dream has a floor of cold flagstones. A library — shelves climbing into dark, long tables, one lamp turned low. You are small in it: heavy sleeves hang past your hands, a student's robe too big for you. You know this room the way you know a road walked in childhood, and in the dream there are no holes — everything missing simply *is* here, unquestioned. Only the letters betray it: every spine bears writing, and none of it will hold still to be read.

A book lies open before you. Its pages make your chest cold. You notice you are already crying; the dream comes with the tears included, worn smooth, like a stair used for centuries.

Then warmth, between the shelves, the way morning comes. It stands eight feet tall in the narrow aisle, eightfold wings folded tight to fit, four faces without eyes — a thought the sun is having about this small cold room.

You stand, and the boyhood falls off you like a coat. The dream reaches for the man you are meant to rise into — there is a shape there, gold-flecked at the eyes — and finds nothing to dress you in. You stand as you are. The faceless faces tilt, all four, very slightly.

Somewhere far above the library's ceiling, you feel small bright things leaving you, rising like seeds.

Then the shelves are gone and you hang in its seeing: the world a fleck of rock, dim and settled — and on its skin, one new pinpoint of light. Unsteady. Guttering, flaring, guttering — a lamp in a draught. It sounds, impossibly, like crying.

The qadín regards you. Its wings neither open nor close.

The dream, which knows more than you do, says: *again.*

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

"Why now?" I ask the qadín. "What am I supposed to do?"

**GM:**

It does not speak. Speaking is yours; it does something older. Meaning arrives in you the way weather arrives, and you understand two things at once, whole, the way you understand the dream's library — without the stairs of learning them.

The first: *now* is not its choosing. Your eyes are drawn back to the guttering pinpoint on the world's dim skin — and you see what the flaring is. Not a lamp in a draught. A lamp in small hands, being carried, and the hands do not know how to carry it, and every stumble slops fire over the brim. Each flare is spilling. Somewhere under that light there are things that catch.

The second arrives as your own hands, huge in the dream suddenly, a man's hands, grey-printed — held before you in the posture you put them in on the stair, around the boy's broken thigh. That posture. That, says the meaning, pressed in like a seal into wax. *You are the craft. Go and set what is broken before it knits wrong.*

Then, gently, the way a door closes on a warm room: the sense of the qadín beginning to withdraw — and beneath your feet the flagstones going thin as ice over dark water, the waking world rising under them. Cold. A smell of stone and warm ash. And through it, faint, from somewhere outside the dream — the sound of a goat's bell, and it is not moving the way goats move.

**GM:**

The escaped gold ignores you. What's out is out — like calling breath back, like unspilling water. It thins and is gone.

But going inward you find what the sleep did. The aperture — the hair-crack the sea leans on — is calmer this morning, and when you put your attention on it, the way you'd press a tongue to a loose tooth, the leak thins. Your next breath comes out almost clean. It isn't sealing; it's holding — a cupped hand around a flame. The moment your attention slips, the shine creeps back into your breath. Held wrong, it aches, high in the chest.

So: it can be done, at the price of never quite thinking about anything else. Cloth would be cheaper.

The girl watches you breathe at nothing with an expression she'd give a man talking to his own hands.

"When you're done," she says.

The gold flecks have moved. They sit higher in the milky face now, turned up toward you, the way eyes turn under closed lids.

Cloth hits your shoulder from behind — her outer shawl, thrown, coarse brown wool smelling of goat. "Against your debt," she says, thin-voiced. She's come halfway down the bank, pole forgotten in her hand.

"That face." She says it flat, making herself. "Not finished, but I can see where it's going. It's going to be yours. You want to tell me again about the lost wanderer?"

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

**GM:**

You kneel, and put your bare fingers to the unfinished face.

Warm. Not like stone in the sun — like a sleeping body, blood-warm, though nothing in it can have blood. Under your fingertips the surface is smooth as the inside of a shell.

It presses up into your hand. Small, blind, certain — the way an infant roots, the way a cat leans into a palm. The whole man-length of it tilts a fraction toward you under the cloak.

And where your fingers rest, the milk of it clears. A patch the size of your hand turns to glass, and through it you see the light inside — not a grain, here; a slow rolling seam of it, the width of a wrist — and the light steadies, the guttering smooths, the way the drop steadied near you.

Then you feel the pull. Faint, gentle, absolutely unashamed: it is drinking you. A thread of your light, drawn down through your fingertips into that steadying seam. It doesn't take; it nurses. And the ache of it isn't pain at all — that's the frightening thing. It feels like being needed.

Up the bank, the girl says, quietly, "Your breath's gone gold again."

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

"I'm going to do something," I say. "This ... creature, it needs something I have. You should stay back."

**GM:**

The girl doesn't argue. You hear her boots go back up the scree, and the bell, and then stillness.

You open the gate.

Gently — a hair, then another hair, your attention wrapped around the crack like both hands on a valve. The sea on the far side is not violent. That's the horror of it: it's *willing*. It rises to the opening the way water rises to a hole, patient, total, and the first thread through is pure relief — the ache in your chest unclenching, pressure equalizing, and some low voice in you sighs *at last* and reaches to open it the rest of the way.

You hold at two hairs' width. It costs more than lifting the brick did.

The light goes down your arm and out through your fingers, gold under the skin, and the creature drinks — no longer nursing now, feeding, greedy as any newborn. Under the cloak the whole shape brightens. And it *builds*. Knuckles rise on the hands like frost forming, fast. The feet articulate. The chest deepens between one breath and the next. The face —

The face comes up under your palm like bread proving: cheekbones, an old scar over the brow of it, a mouth. Lips. Lids, fine as anything ever made, over the two gold flecks.

Somewhere in you, quietly, a payment is taken. A shelf goes empty. You can't see which one from here.

You shut the gate — and shutting it is like turning your back on rest forever; you do it anyway.

The eyes open.

They're gold-flecked, and grey, and they are looking at you the way you looked at the qadín.

It looks at you again. Grey eyes, gold-flecked, an old scar through one brow that you have no memory of earning.

**GM:**

Your fingers go up under the mask-cloth's edge and find it at once: a fine old ridge through your right brow, healed hard, older than memory by definition. Your own face is a country you've never seen. It copied you true — that's all. You wear a wound you cannot account for, and now so does it.

It takes the correction the way a child takes one, without argument: draws the cloak around its shoulders and over its head, so only the face shows. Its breath doesn't steam in the cold, you notice. The air likes it less than it likes you.

"Iron," it repeats, filing the word away. Then, looking at the rust-colored slopes, the dead thorn, the dust: "This place is made of it. Why would you wake anything *here*?"

Stones turn above you. The girl comes down the bank sideways, goat abandoned up top, and stops a spear-length away. She looks the creature over — the cloak, the borrowed face — with her jaw set hard against whatever her legs want to do.

"It talks with your mouth," she says to you. "Fine. It's yours to feed, not mine. Can it walk? Because between the stars falling and that light-show you just made, half the west country will be coming to look, and I'd rather not be here when they do."

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

"I need ..." I take a deep breath. "I need a place to rest, and eat, and clothes. With this ... brother, here. Can you help me? I will ... owe you."

**GM:**

"'Brother,'" the girl repeats, flat. The creature turns the word over silently, mouthing it once, and seems to decide it will keep it.

She looks between you — the same face twice, once ruined and once new — and does her arithmetic. It takes longer this time.

"Not the steading. My uncle sees either of you, he'll be at the cloister-road shrine by noon telling everything he knows to anyone in grey." She points the pole southwest, along the fold of the hills. "There's the summer shieling, ours, two hours' walk. Stone hut, turf roof, hearth. Nobody goes up before shearing. I can bring bread and a blanket and my father's old clothes, and nobody counts what I carry."

A beat.

"And you'll owe me. Not a favor — a *debt*, named later, and you'll pay it when I name it. Both of you say it. I've heard that things that talk gold are held by their word."

Behind her, far off, the mountain stands split against the morning, and down on its skirts you can see what she means: tiny movements on the cloister road, a line of dark figures, and sun catching on something that flashes like polished iron, coming west around the mountain's foot.

The goat bleats once, up on the bank, impatient.

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

"What is your name?" I ask.

**GM:**

"I told you—" She stops. Reconsiders. This is a negotiation now, and names are goods like anything else.

"Whin," she says finally. "It's what they call me — the thornbush, because I'm no one's flower. It's a wear-name; it'll take the weight. My born name you don't get, not while your breath does that."

She plants the pole butt-down in the stones, businesslike.

"Whin, of Harrick's steading under Coldshoulder. Now the debt, like I said. Both of you, out loud. Then we walk."

The creature looks at you — checking, the way a child checks before answering a stranger — and when you don't stop it, it says, in your clean lost voice: "I owe Whin a debt, to be named by her. I will pay it."

The words don't hang gold in the air. They do something else: for half a breath the seam of light in its chest pulses brighter, once, like a knot being pulled tight.

Whin sees it too. Her eyes go narrow, and then, softly: "There. *That's* how you hold things that shine."

She turns to you, waiting.

**Hannu (the Sealed One):**

"I owe Whin a debt, to be named by her. I will pay it. This I swear by the brightness that lies beyond."

**GM:**

The words leave you and the gate leans.

Hard, once — the whole sea shifting its weight against your two-hairs' crack like something vast turning over in sleep at the sound of its own name. You hold. It settles. But your next three breaths come out gold through the cloth, and high in your chest there's a new sensation, thin and definite as a wire: the oath, seated. You have sworn by something that heard you.

Whin steps back a pace. She wanted a debt; she can see she's been given a covenant, and for a moment she looks what she is — sixteen, out on the scree, alone with two of whatever you are. Then she picks up the pole and covers it with motion.

"Right. Walk."

The morning goes to broken hills, goat-paths, dead ground. The creature walks badly for a hundred steps, learning feet — then it finds your gait and wears it, and after that watching it walk is like being followed by your own shadow. Whin ranges ahead with the goat. For the first time since waking, the tilt in your chest and your path lie in the same direction, and the walking feels like a held thing set down.

Two hours on, in the crook of a shoulder of hill: the shieling. Dry-stone, turf roof gone green, a door of silvered wood on leather hinges, one window shuttered blind. A fold for the goats, empty. Silence, and the long land southwest, hazed.

Whin lifts the door-latch with two fingers, and stops.

"Someone's been in," she says.
